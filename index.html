1.Abstract

The discovery of that the universe is expanding changed our understanding towards cosmology. Mathematics provides the tools to descried this expansion, from the geometry of spacetime to equations governing cosmic evolution. This article tells us about the mathematical components underlying our expanding universe. Using general relativity as the foundation, the Friedmann-Lemaître-Robertson-Walker (FLRW) metric models the large-scale structure of spacetime. The scale factor describes how distance grows with time that links directly to Hubble’s Law, which relates to decline velocity to distance. The Friedmann equation the connects expansion to energy density, curvature, and dark energy. Observational proof-such as redshift measurements, the cosmic microwaves background, and supernova surveys-confirms these mathematical predictions. Understanding these equations is essential for interpreting the past and the future behaviour of the universe. This article summarizes the mathematical framework behind cosmic expansion.

2.Introduction

2.1 Evidence for Expansion

The idea of an expanding universe originates from Edwin Hubble’s 1929 observations that galaxies appear to be moving away from us at speeds relative to their distances (Hubble, 1929). This linear relationship provided the first quantitative evidence against the previously assumed static universe. Later analyses confirmed that this recession is not due to galaxies moving through space, but rather space itself expanding (Harrison, 2000). These measurements inspired mathematicians and physicists to develop models that could describe such large-scale behaviour (Harrison, 2000).

2.2 Geometry and the Metric

The mathematics used to describe the universe is based on general relativity, Einstein’s theory that gravity results from the curvature of spacetime (Weinberg, 2008). To model the universe, cosmologists assume homogeneity and isotropy at large scales, a principle known as the cosmological principle (Peacock, 1999).
Under these assumptions, the spacetime geometry is described by the FLRW metric:
d2s2 = - c2 d t2 + a (t) 2 ( d r2 1-kr2 + r2 dΩ2 )
where a(t)is the scale factor, and kdetermines spatial curvature. (Ryden, 2017) explains that this metric allows cosmologists to calculate how distances evolve over time as the universe expands.This metric introduces three possible global geometries—positive curvature (closed), zero curvature (flat), and negative curvature (open) (Ryden, 2017). Modern observations from the Planck satellite show the universe is extremely close to flat, implying k≈0 (Planck Collaboration, 2020).

2.3 The Scale Factor

The scale factor a(t)is a mathematical function that describes how the size of the universe changes with time (Ryden, 2017). Distances between galaxies scale proportionally with a(t). When a(t)=1, cosmologists define this as the present day (Ryden, 2017). A smaller value indicates that distances were proportionally smaller in the past emphasising that the scale factor provides a natural way to relate wavelengths of light to redshift(Ryden, 2017):
1+z = 1 a ( tem )
This equation indicates that the universe’s expansion stretches light, producing the redshift observed in distant galaxies (Harrison, 2000).

2.4 Hubble’s Law

Hubble discovered that a galaxy’s recessional velocity(v) is proportional to its distance d (Hubble, 1929):
v = H0 d
where H_0is the Hubble constant. This constant represents the present-day expansion rate of the universe. According to (Weinberg, 2008), Hubble’s Law is a direct result of the FLRW metric when galaxies are carried away by the stretching of space. The modern value of H_0, as reported by the (Planck Collaboration, 2020), is around 67.4 km s⁻¹ Mpc⁻¹, although other measurements yield slightly higher values, leading to the well-known “Hubble tension”. A common visual analogy is the expansion of dots on the surface of an inflating balloon. The dots move away from each other not because they are travelling across the surface, but because the surface itself expands.

2.5 The Friedmann Equations

The Friedmann equations are the mathematical heart of cosmology. Derived from Einstein’s field equations, they relate the expansion rate of the universe to its energy contents.

The first Friedmann equation is:

a. a 2 = 8πG 3 ρ - kc2 a2 + Λc2 3
where:
  • ρ is the total energy density,
  • k is curvature,
  • Λ is the cosmological constant representing dark energy.

The second Friedmann equation describes the acceleration of expansion:

a.. a = - 4πG 3 ( ρ + 3p c2 ) + Λc2 3
(Peacock, 1999) explains that matter, radiation, and dark energy influence expansion differently because each has different pressure properties. Matter slows expansion, radiation opposes it more strongly, and dark energy accelerates it. Observations show the universe’s expansion is currently accelerating, meaning dark energy dominates the energy density (Planck Collaboration, 2020).

2.6 Modern Cosmology and Observations

Modern cosmological models rely on combining the Friedmann equations with high-precision observations. One of the most important datasets is the cosmic microwave background (CMB), a relic radiation field that preserves conditions from the early universe (Weinberg, 2008). Measurements from the Planck mission refine parameters such as the density of matter, the curvature, and the Hubble constant. These mathematical tools reveal a coherent history of the cosmos: an early period of rapid inflation, followed by matter-dominated slowing, and finally the dark-energy-driven acceleration observed today.

3.Conclusion

The expansion of the universe is fundamentally a mathematical story. The combination of general relativity, the FLRW metric, the scale factor, and the Friedmann equations provides a powerful framework for describing how the cosmos evolves. Observational evidence—from Hubble’s discovery to modern CMB measurements—aligns closely with these mathematical predictions. Understanding the expanding universe therefore requires not only astronomy, but also deep mathematical reasoning. As measurements become more precise, particularly regarding the Hubble constant and dark energy, the mathematics behind the expanding universe will continue to evolve, offering new insights into the fate and structure of the cosmos.
